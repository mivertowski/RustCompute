\begin{abstract}
The actor model has shaped fifty years of concurrent and distributed
systems design~\citep{hewitt1973actors,agha1986actors}, yet its
classical formulations assume CPU execution: preemptive scheduling,
heap-allocated mailboxes, and operating-system-mediated supervision.
GPU programming has evolved in parallel along an orthogonal axis,
treating the device as a coprocessor that executes ephemeral kernels
launched by the host.  The two paradigms have remained largely
disjoint.  Recent hardware advances—NVIDIA Hopper's Thread Block
Clusters and Distributed Shared Memory~(DSMEM), cooperative kernel
launches with grid-wide synchronization, stream-ordered asynchronous
allocation~\citep{nvidia2022hopper,nvidia2023cooperative}—remove the
last technical obstacles to a different model: \emph{persistent
GPU actors} that live on the device across the lifetime of the
application, exchange messages through device-side queues without
host mediation, and recover from faults under supervisor-driven
restart policies.  No formal account of this model has appeared in
the literature.

We present \system{}, a Rust-based runtime that realizes persistent
GPU actors, and the first formal model of their semantics.  Our
contributions are:
\textbf{(i)}~a small-step operational semantics for the actor
lifecycle (\Dormant{}\,\(\to\)\,\Initializing{}\,\(\to\)\,\Active{}\,
\(\to\)\,\Draining{}\,\(\to\)\,\Terminated{}, with \Failed{} as a
fault attractor) that integrates with the GPU's SIMT execution
model and grid-wide cooperative synchronization;
\textbf{(ii)}~a kernel-to-kernel (K2K) messaging calculus with
formal delivery semantics (at-least-once, per-(sender,\,destination)
ordering, hop-bounded routing) and an explicit dead-letter discipline
that distinguishes overflow drops from invalid-target drops, policy
evictions, and idempotency rejections;
\textbf{(iii)}~a Hybrid Logical Clock (HLC) integration that
provides causal ordering across actors on a single device and is
preserved across supervisor-driven restart, with a structural proof
that snapshot-restore preserves causality;
\textbf{(iv)}~a tenant-isolation model in which K2K sub-brokers are
keyed on a two-tuple audit boundary (\(\mathit{org\_id},
\mathit{engagement\_id}\)) and cross-tenant flow is forbidden by
construction, with an information-flow argument that the supervisor's
routing function admits no cross-tenant trace;
\textbf{(v)}~a multi-GPU actor migration protocol with
phase-machine semantics and proofs of atomic-swap, no-message-loss,
FIFO-across-cutover, and state-consistency, plus a structural
guarantee that NVLink-paired actors never fall back to the
host-staged path;
\textbf{(vi)}~empirical validation on NVIDIA H100~NVL silicon: 75\,ns
mapped-memory command injection (8{,}698\(\times\) faster than
\texttt{cuLaunchKernel}), 5.54\,M~messages/second sustained
throughput with a coefficient of variation of 0.05\% over 60~seconds,
and 0.628\,\(\mu\)s cluster-level synchronization
(2.98\(\times\) faster than grid-wide synchronization).  The \TLA{}
specifications backing each formal claim are open and machine-checked.

The model and its proofs subsume three apparently unrelated design
patterns observed in production GPU systems: persistent kernels for
reduced launch overhead~\citep{gupta2012persistent,aila2009raytrace},
device-side work queues for producer–consumer pipelines, and
supervisor trees for fault containment.  We argue that
\emph{persistent GPU actors} is the unifying abstraction these
patterns approximate.  We close by sketching how the model extends
to multi-tenant, multi-engagement workloads in regulated domains
(audit, compliance, safety-critical reasoning) where formal
cross-tenant isolation is itself a deliverable, not just an
implementation detail.
\end{abstract}
